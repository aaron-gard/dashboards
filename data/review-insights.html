💡 Strategic Implications

1. Myer IS the physical network. 52 of 64 locations are Myer concessions. The online halo is really a Myer halo. Any reduction in Myer presence directly erodes online sales density.

2. Canberra outperforms. ACT punches well above population weight. Three stores cover a concentrated, affluent market. Top postcodes: 2913, 2611, 2602, 2620, 2617 — all $40K+.

3. Distance = higher AOV, lower frequency. 100km+ customers spend 16% more per order but buy 4x less frequently. These are intentional, considered purchases, not proximity-driven.

4. Dark zones. NT ($57K) and TAS ($147K) have zero Review stores. Online performance is structurally capped without physical presence to anchor awareness.

5. Boutique scarcity. Only 7 brand-controlled touchpoints vs 52 Myer-controlled. If brand experience drives LTV, the surface area is thin and dependent on a single wholesale partner.

6. Recommended catchments are high-demand corridors. All five recommended catchments show strong online demand far above the portfolio average. These aren’t just underserved markets, they’re markets where demand is compounding without any physical investment.

7. Income validates positioning. NW Sydney’s $123K/yr median household income is 28% above all other catchments. Combined with the highest demand-per-capita ($0.72), this signals a premium customer base already self-selecting into the brand — a physical store would accelerate, not create, demand.

AOV & Competitor Density

AOV decreases modestly with competitor density: $340 → $297 from zero to 30+ competitors. This reflects proximity-driven purchasing in dense areas (lower per-order spend, higher frequency) vs intentional destination shopping in sparse areas.

AOV: 0 competitors$340
AOV: 6–15 competitors$309

The 13% AOV decline is small relative to the 2x demand uplift — net revenue is far higher in competitor-dense areas.

Key Finding: No Saturation Signal

The most saturated postcodes (30–50 competitors within 10km) are all in Melbourne inner-east and Sydney North Shore — and Review already has stores in every one of them. These are established fashion precincts (Chadstone, Chatswood, South Yarra, Doncaster) where 9–10 of 10 competitor brands coexist. The data shows no evidence of competitive cannibalisation — demand in these zones averages $8–18K per postcode.

Key Finding: White Space Validation

The strongest white space signal is where competitors have opened but Review hasn’t. These areas show competitors have already validated the customer base:

• Wollongong (2500): $14.5K demand, 6 competitor stores (4 brands) — 48km from nearest Review
• Blacktown (2148): $15.5K demand, 7 competitors (2 brands) — 10km from nearest Review
• NW Sydney (2767): $11.4K demand, 8 competitors (3 brands) — 13km from nearest Review
• Ingleburn (2567): $20K demand, 5 competitors (3 brands) — 22km from nearest Review
Key Finding: Canberra Anomaly Persists

Canberra’s top postcodes (2913, 2914, 2620) have zero competitors within 10km yet generate $40–55K demand each. This reinforces the Myer-halo thesis — Review’s performance here is driven by its own store network (3 locations), not by fashion precinct agglomeration. Canberra is the only market where Review succeeds without the competitor ecosystem.

📈 Methodology

Ranked by composite score: catchment demand (40%), target demo density - women 25-54 (25%), competitive white space (20%), household income (15%). Catchment demand calculated by aggregating all postcodes with CY2025 online demand (Polar Analytics) within 15km of each recommended centre, using ABS postcode centroid coordinates and haversine distance. Nearest store distance measured to existing Review network (64 locations). Demographics sourced from ABS Census 2021 QuickStats (POA-level): population, median household income, median age, and age/gender distribution. Growth corridors will have materially higher current populations - these are conservative 2021 baselines.
