↔ Key Findings

Store closures are not driving online growth. Online revenue through the loyalty program has declined in parallel with store closures ‚Äî from $34.3M (CY22) to $18.8M (CY25). The "halo" works in reverse: losing physical presence appears to reduce online demand, not redirect it.
Physical signups declining faster than closures explain. Even open stores are seeing fewer signups ‚Äî total physical signups dropped from ~46K/quarter (2022) to ~27K/quarter (2025). Store closures accelerate but don't fully explain the trend.
Boutique closures hit harder than Myer concessions. Boutiques like Fountain Gate ($1.9M/yr), Melbourne Central ($1.4M/yr), and Highpoint ($1.2M/yr) had significantly higher revenue per location than closed Myer concessions.
No evidence of demand migration. When a store closes, the revenue doesn't appear at nearby locations or online. It largely evaporates ‚Äî consistent with the halo effect data showing physical presence generates demand rather than just capturing it.

Highpoint is the exception ‚Äî and it proves the rule. Highpoint's catchment (3031-3033) overlaps with several other active Review stores in inner-west Melbourne. When nearby stores maintain the halo, some demand can shift. Where no other store covers the gap, demand evaporates.
